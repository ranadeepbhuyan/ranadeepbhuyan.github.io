The 45 / 5 / 5 Rule

45 minutes of focused work → 5 minutes of real rest → 5 minutes planning your next task → repeat. Simple. Powerful. No phone involved.

5 min PLAN
45 min FOCUS
5 min REST
📱➡️🚪
Step 0 — Phone Leaves the Room

Not on silent. Not face down. In another room. This is the single most important step. No negotiation.

🎯
Step 1 — Pick ONE Task

Not "study math." Something specific: "Solve exercises 4-8 from Chapter 6." Small and clear beats big and vague.

⏱️
Step 2 — Set a 45-Minute Timer

Use the Timer tab, a wall clock, or a kitchen timer. Not your phone. 45 minutes — that's it. Not forever.

🔥
Step 3 — Work Through the Friction

Around 10-15 mins in, your brain will scream for a distraction. This is normal. It passes in about 2 minutes if you don't feed it. Push through — this is where real learning happens.

🌿
Step 4 — Take a REAL 5-Min Break

Timer rings → stand up, walk around, stretch, drink water, look out the window. No screens. Let your brain actually rest.

📋
Step 5 — Plan the Next Task (5 min)

Before diving back in, take 5 minutes to write down exactly what you'll work on next. "What's the one thing I need to do in the next 45 minutes?" This removes decision paralysis when the next round starts.

🔁
Step 6 — Repeat & Reward

Go again. 2-3 rounds = 90-135 mins of real focused work. That's more than most students get in an entire evening. Check the Rewards tab for how to celebrate your wins.

📧
Step 7 — Showcase Your Progress

Any time you want, send a snapshot of your weekly goals and study blocks to your tutor, parents, or yourself. Sharing progress keeps you accountable and makes your hard work visible. Open Update →

⚡ The Math That Matters

2 focused rounds = ~1.5 hours of real work.
3 hours of "studying" with your phone nearby = maybe 30 mins of actual focus.
Less time. Better results. More free time after.

Walk to the kitchen, drink water
Movement + hydration resets attention
Step outside for 2 minutes
Fresh air + distance from the desk
Stretch or do 10 jumping jacks
Gets blood flowing to the brain
Stare out a window or at the ceiling
Feels boring — that's how you know it's working

📋 Planning Break (the secret weapon)

The 5-minute planning phase is what separates this from ordinary study. Here's what to do:

Write down what you just finished
One line — "Completed Ch.6 problems 4-8." Gives you a sense of progress.
Identify exactly what's next
Be specific: "Read pages 50-55 and highlight key terms" not "do more biology."
Note anything you're stuck on
Write it down so it doesn't drain mental energy during the next round.

🔑 The Test

After your break, do you feel ready to work again — or do you want more break? If you want more, it wasn't a real break. It was entertainment your brain is now craving more of.

🏆 Why Rewards Matter

Your brain learns to repeat behaviours that feel rewarding. If studying always ends in guilt or exhaustion, your brain will avoid it. If it ends with something genuinely enjoyable, your brain starts associating focus with good outcomes. The trick: rewards must come after the work, never during.

Reward Tiers — Earn as You Go

⭐
After 1 Block (45 min)
First reward = real rest. Always.
Take your 5-min real break — stretch, walk, water, fresh air. Then treat yourself: a favourite snack, play one song loud, doodle for fun, make yourself a nice drink.
🌟
After 2 Blocks (~2 hrs)
Rest first, then a medium reward
Take a proper break — go outside, breathe, move your body. Then: call your favourite friend or cousin, play a quick game, go for a walk with music, tidy up your bag or desk for tomorrow, have dessert.
🔥
After 3 Blocks (~3 hrs)
You've earned serious rest + a big reward
Rest your mind fully first — you've worked hard. Then ask yourself what you'd like to do — define a new time-boxed reward for yourself, 30 min of gaming, go out with friends, order something you've wanted online.
💎
Weekly Streak (5+ days)
The big one — consistency deserves celebration
Movie night, a new book or game, a meal out, a full free afternoon with zero guilt — you earned every second.

💡 Smart Reward Rules

Reward AFTER, never during
"I'll watch this after 2 blocks" works. "I'll watch while studying" destroys focus.
Decide the reward before you start
Knowing what's waiting for you makes it easier to push through the hard parts.
Time-box screen rewards
If the reward is gaming or a show, set a timer for that too. 30 min of guilt-free fun, then stop.
Physical rewards hit different
A walk, a snack, a shower, shooting hoops — these feel more rewarding than screens after deep focus.
Never skip the reward
If you did the work, take the reward. Skipping it teaches your brain that effort doesn't pay off.

🧠 The Science

Dopamine (the "motivation chemical") doesn't just respond to pleasure — it responds to anticipated pleasure. When you pair focused work with a reliable reward afterward, your brain starts releasing dopamine before you even sit down to study. You're literally rewiring yourself to want to focus.

🧠 The Friction Moment Is Normal

About 10-15 minutes into hard work, your brain creates an urgent "I need a break" feeling. This is not exhaustion — it's your brain testing whether you'll give it easy dopamine. If you ride it out for ~2 minutes, it fades and focus deepens.

🎮 You're Being Played

Short-form video apps hire hundreds of engineers whose only job is to keep you scrolling. The algorithm studies what makes YOU specifically unable to stop. It's not a fair fight — so don't fight it. Remove the option entirely during study time.

📝 Be Specific About Tasks

"Study biology" → brain panics, doesn't know where to start, reaches for phone. "Read pages 42-48 and write 5 bullet points" → brain has a clear path. Specific tasks feel less overwhelming.

🏆 Track Your Wins

Each completed 45-minute block is a win. Use the Tracker tab or a simple tally on paper. Seeing your streaks builds momentum and makes the next session easier to start.

😤 Bad Sessions Still Count

A 45-minute block where you struggled and felt confused is still better than 3 hours of scrolling. Learning feels uncomfortable. That's not a sign it's not working — it's the sign that it IS.

🕐 Start With Just One Block

Don't promise yourself 3 hours. Promise yourself one 45-minute block. That's it. After that, decide if you want another. Most of the time, starting is the hardest part.

📋 Planning Prevents Procrastination

The 5-minute planning phase isn't wasted time — it's what stops you from opening your phone "just to figure out what to do next." When the next round starts and you already know exactly what to work on, there's no gap for distraction to sneak in.

✈️ Go Offline on Your Mac

Macs don't have a single "Airplane Mode" button like iPhones. But you can go fully offline in seconds by turning off Wi-Fi and Bluetooth manually. Do this before every study session.

Method 1 — Menu Bar (Fastest)
Step 1: Turn off Wi-Fi
Click the Wi-Fi icon in the top-right corner of your screen → Toggle the switch OFF. The icon will appear with a slash through it.
Step 2: Turn off Bluetooth
Click the Control Center icon (looks like two sliders) in the top-right → Click Bluetooth → Toggle it OFF.
Done!
You're now fully offline. No notifications, no temptation, no distractions. This page still works — it's already loaded.
Method 2 — System Settings
Step 1:
Click the Apple menu (top-left corner) → Select System Settings.
Step 2:
Click Wi-Fi in the sidebar → Toggle the switch OFF.
Step 3:
Click Bluetooth in the sidebar → Toggle the switch OFF.

⏰ Make It a Ritual

Before every study session:
1. Phone goes in another room
2. Open this Focus page on your laptop
3. Turn off Wi-Fi and Bluetooth (takes 5 seconds)
4. Start the timer

When you're done studying, just reverse the steps to go back online.

💡 Why This Works

Going offline removes the possibility of distraction, so your brain stops wasting energy resisting it. You don't need willpower to avoid YouTube if YouTube literally can't load. Save your willpower for the hard problems.
